Bias in AI Systems ▼

What is bias? AI models trained on biased data will make biased decisions. This can perpetuate or amplify existing inequalities.

Gender Bias
AI trained on male-dominated data may favor men in hiring, lending, or healthcare decisions.

Racial Bias
Historical data with racial disparities can lead to discriminatory AI systems.

Age Bias
AI may discriminate based on age in hiring, insurance, or credit decisions.

Language Bias
AI trained on English performs worse for other languages and dialects.

Real-world impact:

  • Amazon's hiring AI was biased against women
  • Facial recognition works worse for people with darker skin
  • Healthcare algorithms were biased against Black patients

✓ What to do: Be aware of bias risk. Question AI recommendations in high-stakes decisions. Audit AI systems regularly. Use diverse training data.

Copyright & Plagiarism ▼

The Issue: AI models are trained on copyrighted material scraped from the internet. When AI generates text, is it plagiarism?

Current situation (as of 2024):

  • AI is trained on copyrighted works without explicit permission
  • AI-generated text is not automatically copyrighted
  • If AI output closely matches copyrighted work, it could be plagiarism
  • Legal cases are ongoing (authors suing OpenAI, Google, etc.)

⚠️ The Concern: If you use AI-generated content and present it as your own without disclosure, you may be passing off copyright-infringing content.

Safe practices:

  • Use AI as a starting point for editing, not final output
  • Substantially rewrite AI-generated content
  • Disclose AI usage in your work
  • For published work, check publisher guidelines on AI usage
  • Never pass off AI content as your own original creation
Transparency & Disclosure ▼

When must you disclose AI usage?

HIGH disclosure priority:

  • Academic writing: Disclosing AI usage is becoming required
  • Professional communication: Business emails, proposals if it's AI-generated
  • Published content: Blog posts, articles, news
  • Legal/Medical: High-stakes decisions must involve human review, not just AI

LOWER disclosure priority:

  • Personal use (brainstorming for yourself)
  • Internal work that won't be shared publicly
  • Using AI for analysis or research (not AI-generated output)

💡 Best Practice: Default to transparency. If in doubt, disclose AI usage. It builds trust and sets good expectations.

Fairness & Accountability ▼

AI should be fair and accountable. If AI makes a decision that affects people, someone should be responsible.

Fairness principle: AI systems should treat all groups fairly. Watch for disparate impact—outcomes that disadvantage certain groups even if not intentional.

Accountability principle: Humans should make final decisions on consequential matters. Don't let AI decide alone on hiring, loans, criminal justice, or medical treatment.

Explainability: If AI makes an important decision, you should be able to explain why. If you can't, reconsider using AI for that decision.

Practical checklist:

  • Who is impacted by this AI decision?
  • Could this decision harm a specific group?
  • Is a human reviewing the AI recommendation?
  • Can I explain why the AI made this choice?
  • Is there an appeals process if someone disagrees?
Environmental Impact ▼

Training large AI models uses significant energy. This has environmental implications.

The numbers:

  • Training GPT-3 used ~1,287 MWh of electricity
  • That's equivalent to ~130 homes' yearly electricity usage
  • Carbon footprint: significant, though improving

⚠️ Consider: Using AI services has a carbon footprint. Some providers use renewable energy, others don't. It's not harmful per se, but worth considering for large-scale applications.

What you can do:

  • Use efficient models (Claude Haiku, GPT-3.5) when appropriate
  • Batch requests to reduce API calls
  • Support providers using renewable energy
  • Don't overuse AI for trivial tasks
Responsible AI Best Practices ▼

How to use AI responsibly in your work:

  1. Audit for bias. Test AI on diverse groups. Watch for disparate impact.
  2. Keep humans in the loop. For important decisions, AI recommends; human decides.
  3. Disclose AI usage. Be transparent about where AI was used.
  4. Protect privacy. Don't put confidential data in free tiers.
  5. Verify facts. AI can hallucinate. Check critical information.
  6. Be fair. Don't use AI to discriminate. Actively look for bias.
  7. Educate your team. Make sure everyone understands AI limitations and ethics.
  8. Have a policy. Establish organizational guidelines for AI usage.

Key Takeaways
  • Bias is real. AI trained on biased data perpetuates bias. Audit and test for disparate impact.
  • Disclose AI usage. Especially for published content, academic work, and professional communication.
  • Don't pass off AI work as your own. Substantially rewrite and credit AI assistance.
  • Keep humans in control. For high-stakes decisions (hiring, medical, legal), AI assists; humans decide.
  • Protect privacy. Never paste confidential data into free tiers.
  • Fairness matters. Design AI systems to be fair across all groups.
  • Transparency builds trust. Be open about AI's role in your work.
