Learn from real-world errors. Prevent costly AI failures in your work.

❌ Trusting Hallucinations Without Verification

The Problem: AI can confidently state false facts. You ask "What is X?" and AI gives a plausible-sounding answer that's completely wrong.

✓ The Solution:

  • Always verify critical facts independently
  • Use AI for drafting, brainstorming, analysis—not as a truth source
  • Ask AI: "Are you certain about this?" or "What's your confidence level?"
  • Cross-reference with authoritative sources
❌ Sharing Confidential Data

The Problem: Pasting proprietary code, customer data, or business secrets into ChatGPT's free tier. OpenAI may use this for training models or might leak it.

✓ The Solution:

  • Use paid API or business plans for sensitive data
  • Anonymize data before pasting (remove names, dates, IDs)
  • Use self-hosted models for maximum control
  • Treat free tiers as public—never paste confidential content
❌ Using AI for Real-Time Information

The Problem: Asking "What's happening in the news today?" or "What's the current price of Bitcoin?" AI has a knowledge cutoff—it doesn't know about today.

✓ The Solution:

  • Use ChatGPT with web browsing for current events
  • Use Perplexity AI for real-time search
  • Always check the knowledge cutoff date
  • For current data, search the web first, then ask AI to analyze
❌ Poor Prompt Structure

The Problem: Vague prompts get vague outputs. Asking "Write something about AI" gives generic, unhelpful responses.

✓ The Solution:

  • Always use the CLEAR framework (Lesson 3)
  • Specify: audience, length, format, tone, constraints
  • Show examples of what you want
  • Test and iterate if first output isn't perfect
❌ Not Iterating Enough

The Problem: First output is mediocre, so you give up or use it as-is. People forget that iteration is the key to great AI outputs.

✓ The Solution:

  • Plan to iterate 2-3 times minimum
  • Use follow-ups: "Make it more concise," "Add more examples"
  • Provide specific feedback: "That section was weak, strengthen this part"
  • Think of it as a collaborative writing process
❌ Misusing AI for Tasks It Can't Do

The Problem: Using AI for tasks outside its capabilities (real-time calculations, complex math, logic problems, recent events) and getting wrong answers.

✓ The Solution:

  • Know AI's strengths: reasoning, writing, brainstorming, analysis
  • Know AI's weaknesses: math, current events, deterministic logic
  • For math/calculations, use calculators or spreadsheets
  • Use AI to explain or conceptualize, not to provide final answers
Safe AI Usage Checklist ▼

Before you use AI for important work, check these boxes:

  • Is this data confidential? (If yes, use paid tier/API)
  • Does this need to be 100% accurate? (If yes, plan to verify)
  • Have I used the CLEAR framework?
  • Am I using AI for a task it's good at?
  • Have I planned to iterate and refine?
  • Will I fact-check critical claims?

If you answer "no" to any of these, adjust your approach before using AI output.

Key Takeaways
  • Never trust AI facts blindly. Always verify critical information independently.
  • Confidential data belongs in paid APIs, not free tiers.
  • AI isn't real-time. Use it for analysis, not for current events or live data.
  • Prompts matter. Vague prompts get vague answers. Use CLEAR framework.
  • Iteration is key. First output is rarely perfect—plan to refine.
  • Know AI's limits. Use it for writing/reasoning, not for math/logic.
  • Use a checklist before trusting AI output for important decisions.
