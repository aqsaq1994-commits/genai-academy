What is Generative AI?
Definition & Concept ▼

Generative AI is a category of AI that can create new content based on patterns it learned from training data. If you've used ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, DALL-E, or Midjourney—you've used generative AI.

The key word is "generate." Unlike traditional AI that classifies existing data, generative AI creates novel outputs—essays, code, images, music—that didn't exist before.

Real-World Examples ▼

Real-world examples: ChatGPT writes emails you describe in plain English. DALL-E creates images from text descriptions. GitHub Copilot generates code suggestions. Jukebox composes music. All are generative AI.

How LLMs Actually Work
The Prediction Mechanism ▼

Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT and Claude work through a simple mechanism: predicting the next word.

Here's the process:

  1. You give the model text (a prompt)
  2. The model asks: "Given what came before, what word is statistically most likely to come next?"
  3. It picks that word and adds it to your response
  4. It repeats this process hundreds of times to build a complete response
  5. The result appears as a coherent, human-like essay
Mental Model: Autocomplete on Steroids ▼

💡 Think of it like autocomplete on your phone. LLMs are trained on billions of words from the internet, so they're phenomenally good at predicting what word comes next. This is why they sound intelligent—they're just following statistical patterns very, very well.

Strengths & Limitations
✅ What LLMs Excel At ▼
  • Summarizing text quickly
  • Explaining concepts clearly
  • Brainstorming creative ideas
  • Drafting emails, code, content
  • Finding patterns in data
  • Translating languages
  • Creative writing & storytelling
⚠️ What LLMs Struggle With ▼
  • Real-time information
  • Complex math & calculations
  • Proprietary data they weren't trained on
  • Formal logic reasoning
  • Remembering old conversations
  • Knowing when they're wrong
  • Events after their training date
🚨 The Hallucination Problem ▼

⚠️ Sometimes LLMs generate plausible-sounding but completely false information. They're not trying to deceive—they're just following the "predict the next word" pattern even when they don't actually know the answer. Always verify important facts independently.

Key Takeaways
  • Generative AI creates new content by learning patterns from training data.
  • LLMs work through prediction: they guess the next word based on what came before, repeatedly.
  • Strengths: fast, creative, pattern-matching. Weaknesses: no real-time data, can hallucinate, not truly reasoning.
  • Hallucinations are a real risk: always verify critical information.
  • Your job: know what you're using AI for and verify results appropriately.

Prompt Engineering Basics
What Makes a Good Prompt? ▼

A good prompt has four key elements:

  • Clarity - Be specific and unambiguous
  • Context - Provide relevant background information
  • Constraints - Set expectations (length, format, tone)
  • Examples - Show what you want with examples

Bad: "Write about AI" | Good: "Write a 200-word explanation of how LLMs predict text, targeting non-technical professionals."

The CLEAR Framework ▼

A proven framework for crafting effective prompts:

C - Context
Provide background and situation

L - Language
Set the tone and style (formal, casual, technical)

E - Examples
Show 1-2 examples of desired output

A - Audience
Specify who the output is for

R - Result
Be clear about what you want (format, length, details)

Iteration: The Key to Better Results ▼

Rarely does the first prompt produce perfect results. The secret to great AI outputs is iteration:

  1. Get initial output from your first prompt
  2. Evaluate - What's good? What's missing?
  3. Refine your prompt based on feedback
  4. Repeat until you get the result you want

Pro Tip: Use follow-up prompts like "Make this more concise" or "Add more examples" instead of rewriting from scratch.

Context Engineering
Understanding Context Windows ▼

Every LLM has a context window - the maximum amount of text it can process at once.

  • Claude 3 Opus: 200K tokens (≈150,000 words)
  • ChatGPT-4: 128K tokens (≈96,000 words)
  • Gemini Pro: 32K tokens (≈24,000 words)

Impact: Larger context windows let you paste full documents, conversations, or codebases for AI to analyze.

Effective Context Management ▼

Strategies to manage context effectively:

1. Prioritize Information
Put the most important information first (recency matters)

2. Summarize When Needed
Condense long documents to key points

3. Use Clear Markers
Use headers, bullet points, and dividers for structure

4. Remove Noise
Cut irrelevant information to save context space

Advanced Techniques
Chain-of-Thought Prompting ▼

Ask the AI to "think through" the problem step-by-step.

Example: Instead of "Is Claude a good choice for data analysis?", ask "Walk through the strengths and limitations of Claude for data analysis tasks, then recommend if it's a good fit."

Why it works: Forces the AI to reason through each step, leading to more thoughtful and accurate answers.

Role-Playing & Persona-Based Prompts ▼

Give the AI a role or persona to adopt.

Example: "Act as a technical writer explaining machine learning to non-technical business stakeholders. Use analogies and avoid jargon."

Why it works: Personas help AI match tone, depth, and style to your needs more effectively.

Few-Shot Learning ▼

Provide examples of the pattern you want the AI to follow.

Example: Show 2-3 examples of customer feedback with sentiment labels (Positive/Negative/Neutral), then ask the AI to label new feedback in the same format.

Why it works: Seeing examples helps the AI understand your exact expectations for format, tone, and output.

Key Takeaways
  • Good prompts = better results. Clarity, context, and examples matter.
  • Use the CLEAR framework to structure your prompts consistently.
  • Iteration is your superpower. First output rarely perfect—refine and retry.
  • Context management lets you leverage larger context windows effectively.
  • Advanced techniques (chain-of-thought, role-playing, few-shot) unlock better outputs.
  • Practice and experiment. What works depends on your use case.

Build a Prompt Using CLEAR
Task: You need to generate a product description for an online store. Write a prompt using the CLEAR framework (Context, Language, Examples, Audience, Result). Focus on selling a high-end wireless headphone to tech enthusiasts.
✓ Expert Feedback

What a strong CLEAR prompt includes:

  • Context: "You are an e-commerce product writer" or mention the product type
  • Language: "Write in a tech-savvy, persuasive tone" - specify the style
  • Examples: Show 1-2 example descriptions you like
  • Audience: "for tech enthusiasts aged 25-45" - be specific
  • Result: "Create a 150-word product description that highlights sound quality, noise cancellation, and battery life"

Your prompt likely mentioned most of these. The key is being specific and structured!

2
Improve a Weak Prompt
Task: This prompt is too vague: "Write about machine learning."
Rewrite it to be clear, specific, and actionable. Include context, audience, constraints, and the desired result.
✓ Expert Feedback

A much better prompt would be:

"Write a 300-word explanation of how supervised machine learning works, targeted at business executives with no technical background. Use a simple analogy (like teaching a child) and avoid jargon. Include one real-world example of supervised learning in business."

Key improvements:

  • ✓ Specific length (300 words)
  • ✓ Clear audience (business executives, non-technical)
  • ✓ Specific constraints (use analogy, avoid jargon)
  • ✓ Specific format (explanation + example)
  • ✓ Actionable and measurable

Manage Context Effectively
Task: You want to ask Claude to analyze a 50-page document about company strategy. Your context window is 100K tokens. What's the BEST approach?
✓ Expert Feedback

The best answer is B - Summarize key sections first.

Why?

  • Option A - Works but wastes context space on background material
  • Option B - ✓ BEST: You summarize, paste focused content, get deep insights
  • Option C - Adds an unnecessary step; your summarization is better
  • Option D - Splits context and loses the big picture

Lesson: Your context is precious. Prioritize information, remove noise, and be selective about what you paste.

4
Choose the Right Advanced Technique
Task: You need Claude to evaluate code quality for security vulnerabilities. Which advanced technique is MOST appropriate?
✓ Expert Feedback

The best answer is D - Combine chain-of-thought + few-shot examples.

Why?

  • A (Role-playing) - Good, but not specific enough for security
  • B (Chain-of-thought) - ✓ Good: Forces step-by-step reasoning
  • C (Few-shot) - ✓ Good: Shows patterns of vulnerable vs. secure code
  • D (Both B + C) - ✓ BEST: Combines reasoning + examples for best results

Expert Prompt: "Act as a security expert. Review this code line-by-line for vulnerabilities. Here are examples of vulnerable code (X) vs secure alternatives (Y). Now review this new code:"
